The problem

A single key is a single point of failure.

Traditional custody concentrates control in one private key or one hardware module. Whoever holds it holds everything, and losing it, leaking it, or having it seized is catastrophic and irreversible. Operations make it worse: rotating keys usually means moving every asset to new addresses.

Single key

One secret to steal

A lone private key is the whole castle. Compromise it once and every asset under it is gone, with no recourse.

Custodial

Counterparty exposure

Handing assets to a third-party custodian replaces key risk with counterparty risk: their insolvency or breach becomes yours.

Rotation

Key changes break addresses

Conventional schemes force an on-chain move to new addresses just to rotate a key, disrupting integrations and reconciliation.

Quantum

Identity at long-term risk

The signatures proving who controls an account rest on cryptography a future quantum computer can break.

What we deliver

Threshold custody, no single point of compromise.

Not a key in a vault: a private key that is never whole, split across parties and reconstituted only as signatures, never as a secret.

2-of-3 threshold signing

Three independent shares, any two of which sign. No single party can move funds, and no single party can lose them.

CGGMP21 + FROST

Threshold ECDSA via CGGMP21 and threshold Schnorr via FROST, so the key exists only as distributed shares. This is the frontier, and the hardest part.

HSM attestation

Shares are generated and held under hardware security modules with cryptographic attestation of their integrity.

Rotation without new addresses

Rotate and reshare key material on a schedule while the public addresses stay exactly the same.

Resharing on demand

Add, remove, or refresh signers by resharing, with no asset movement and no change to who can transact.

Post-quantum identity

ML-DSA-based identity and attestation so the proof of who controls an account survives the quantum transition.
