Post-quantum

The hardest problem in security, solved at the frontier

Quantum computers will break the public-key cryptography behind nearly every system online. Adversaries already harvest encrypted data today to decrypt it the moment they can. NIST has finalized the standards, and the migration is no longer optional. It is not just banks: every institution running critical infrastructure has to move, and the deepest parts of that move are the hardest. That is exactly where we work.

Solve the threshold problem

Most vendors stop at swapping in a NIST default. We build post-quantum threshold cryptography: distributed signing with no single point of compromise. This is the frontier, and the hardest part.

Real cryptography, submitted to NIST. Not slideware.

Pulsar, Corona, and Magnetar are our own threshold constructions over the NIST post-quantum standards, ML-DSA (FIPS 204) and SLH-DSA (FIPS 205): all open source, and an active submission to NIST's Multi-Party Threshold Cryptography process.

Research: Corona, our 2-round R-LWE threshold, advanced to rank-k Module-LWE in Pulsar · NIST MPTC

luxfi/pulsar Threshold ML-DSA over Module-LWE (rank-k): 2-round signing with dynamic resharing, verifiable under unmodified FIPS 204. Active NIST MPTC submission. luxfi/magnetar Threshold SLH-DSA (FIPS 205): hash-based post-quantum signatures, the conservative primitive in the suite. luxfi/corona Our first: Ring-LWE 2-round threshold signatures for public networks, with production lifecycle plus permissionless, leaderless DKG.
